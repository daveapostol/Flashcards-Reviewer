1. Agricultural Systems

System Inputs & The Green Revolution

Physical Inputs: Temperature (minimum 5°C for most crops), Rainfall (Rice requires >2000mm), Soil (Rich alluvial soil for heavy cropping).

The Green Revolution (Human Factors):

  • HYVs (High Yield Varieties): Genetic seeds (like IR8 Rice) that mature faster and yield more grain.
  • Mechanization: Use of tractors and harvesters increases efficiency but reduces rural jobs.
  • Chemicals: Large-scale use of inorganic fertilizers and pesticides to ensure high yields.

Classification of Farming

  • Arable: Focus on crops | Pastoral: Focus on livestock.
  • Subsistence: For family survival (often shifting cultivation/nomadic).
  • Commercial: For profit (often monocultures like palm oil).
  • Intensive: High inputs (labor/capital) per unit of small land.
  • Extensive: Low inputs across a large land area.

IGCSE Exam Tip: Comparing Systems

When comparing two photos (like in your midterm):

  • Scale: Large fields vs small fragmented plots.
  • Technology: Presence of machinery vs hand tools/manual labor.
  • Infrastructure: Presence of irrigation canals vs reliance on rain.
  • Commercial: Proximity to transport (trucks) vs family dwellings on the farm.

2. Food Shortages

Case Study: Food Shortages in Darfur, Sudan

Standard 7-Mark Focus

Detailed Causes

  • Natural: Persistent droughts in the Sahel region; soil erosion due to overgrazing; desertification.
  • Political: Conflict/Civil war results in Internally Displaced Persons (IDPs) who cannot farm their land.
  • Economic: Lack of government investment in rural transport; high cost of imported seeds.

Specific Effects

  • Malnutrition: Deficiency diseases such as Marasmus and Kwashiorkor (protein lack).
  • Dependency: Reliance on NGOs and World Food Programme (WFP) aid.
  • Demographic: High infant mortality rates and reduced life expectancy.
  • Cycle of Poverty: Malnourished workers lack the strength to farm, leading to further shortages.

3. Tourism: Impacts

Resource Conflict: Water in Seychelles

A prime example of Natural vs. Human resource management from your course materials:

The Problem:

Seychelles loses 75% of its water through old, leaking pipes. During droughts, tourists complain about "filthy toilets" and lack of water, causing friction with locals who face restrictions.

The Mismanagement:

The government took a $25 million loan for a desalination plant instead of fixing leaks or building reservoirs to catch abundant rainy-season water. This increases national debt without solving the root infrastructure issue.

Key Concepts

  • Multiplier Effect: Revenue flows through the local economy (Tourist → Hotel → Local Farmer).
  • Leakage: When profit leaves the country to foreign TNCs (Total opposite of multiplier).

Environmental Impacts

  • Positive: Revenue used to protect coral reefs and national parks.
  • Negative: Habitat destruction for resorts; pollution from cruise ships; depletion of local water tables.

Case Study: Sustainable Tourism in Dubai, UAE

Solar & Efficiency

Dubai's Sustainable City uses solar power and treats 100% of wastewater for landscape irrigation.

Diversification

Shifting the economy from oil to tourism/knowledge to ensure stability when oil reserves are depleted.

Conservation

The Al Marmoom Reserve protects the desert ecosystem while educating tourists about local biodiversity.

4. Energy Supply

Global Electricity Trends

Billion Kilowatt-hours

Graph Analysis

Identify the trends for the 5-mark question.

  • Coal: Highest source (dominates).
  • Renewables: Steepest gradient (Fastest growth).
  • Gas: Steady increase (Transition fuel).
  • Oil: Stagnant or declining.

Case Study: Energy in Iceland

Geothermal & HEP

Physical Factor: Tectonics

Sitting on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge (Constructive Boundary), Iceland uses geothermal steam to power cities and heat homes/greenhouses directly.

Physical Factor: Relief

Steep mountains and glacial rivers allow for massive HEP (Hydro-Electric Power) plants which provide 70% of their electricity, mainly for the aluminum industry.

Exam Question Help

"Study the Global Electricity Trends. Describe the projected changes in global electricity generation."

"Coal is expected to remain the dominant source, reaching 12,000 billion kWh. However, Renewables are projected to have the fastest growth rate, nearly tripling. In contrast, Oil is expected to decline slightly over the period."
